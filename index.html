These past months I have been struggling and, quite miserably, failing—frequently, publicly, and without much grace. Progress slowed, plans collapsed, energy disappeared. The schedule did not care. Productivity certainly didn’t. And yet, somehow, this is where things started to make sense. This work does not ask how to overcome failure, but how to stay with it long enough to learn from it. How to fail together. How to build something livable from interruption, slowness, and non-mastery. If success is about control, then failure might be about connection. And if that’s true, then maybe failing—fabulously—is not such a bad place to begin.


This research essay explores various practices under the umbrella of queer independent publishing. Its main focus is on contemporary queer (maga)zines (primarily printed or designed to be printed) that came to life from the early 2000s up until today. Yet, I also briefly look back at the history of alternative publishing in the second half of the 20th century to acknowledge the roots of current practices. Coming from a feeling of personal frustration with the corporate/mainstream graphic design world, my struggle to relate to it, and a hope to find my spot within the small/alternative publishing world; my research asks: what queer publishing practices exist today? What is the value of indie publishing for our communities and for the society as a whole? and what’s the point of producing printed matter now, in the times of ubiquitous digital networks and information overflow?


By looking at historical and contemporary examples of queer (maga)zines (in the Amsterdam-based LGBT+ archive and local bookstores), reflecting on the ideas of theorists and practitioners related to the publishing field (e.g. Paul Soulellis’ Queer.Archive.Works, Be Oakley’s GenderFail, Alessandro Ludovico’s Post-Digital Print, Lara Dautun’s Two Stories of Feminist Publishing, Julia Bryan-Wilson’s essay in the book Copy Machine Manifestos: Artists Who Make Zines, etc), making zines myself, organising collaborative zine-making sessions, and testing out different distribution models; I hope to grasp better understanding of various practices of independent queer publishing, situate them within the broader field of contemporary graphic design, and hopefully situate myself somewhere along the way :)

Modern automated systems offer frictionless orientation while guiding users along invisible structural paths. The unpleasant feeling of being lost is treated as a problem to be eliminated, and spaces that produce this sensation are gradually disappearing. If there is no pause, there is no reflection. Autonomy appears to be the in the center, as you are choosing your own content, yet movement takes place along a pre-curated path.
This essay examines the relationship between friction, autonomy, and the labyrinthine self, with particular attention to Jorge Luis Borges’ literary labyrinths and the layered structure of sense of self in Escape to Berlin. Intentional disorientation emerges as a design strategy capable of restoring the possibility of reflection and awareness within increasingly polarized environments.

According to the Greek life of Adam and Eve, before his expulsion from Paradise, Adam received permission to take four kinds of aromatic fragrances to use as early incense offerings. Cinnamon, spikenard, calamus and myrrh.. Among the human senses, smell remains the most elusive and subjective, neither theoretical nor practical sense, often overshadowed by sight and hearing in philosophical, scientific, and aesthetic discourses. It is that the perfume of things has a unique capacity that other senses do not offer: it enters through our nose and searches through folders right from the dusty cupboards of our memory. Smell in this way directly links to emotion and memory, avoiding the brain’s rational filtering systems. It escapes the shaping of things we might think we know, believe or rationalise. Unlike the more theoretical senses such as sight or hearing, we are not confronted to a pushed education through symbolic systems and significance of phenomenons our nose can capture. Through this we are able to create raw and natural relations between our environment and our mind, without this educational input. And as these reactions are not being dominated by rationality and the intellect, they let themselves be guided by emotional responses. Smell, in this sense, functions as a deeply psychological trigger rather than a purely aesthetic experience. Despite these scientific and psychological significances, positioning smell as a subject of interest and uniqueness, perfume, which is the direct medium for the sense of smell, since it can only be sensed through the nose, is most often discussed as an aesthetic or commercial object, rather than a medium capable of carrying memory, meaning, and collective experience. Given the history of perfumes and their original form of use, which I will discuss more thoroughly later, perfume can be associated with a higher state of being and a bridge between our material world on earth and the spiritual heavens. This makes perfume a medium to connect with the supernatural. A medium that does not carry strong symbolism and idealistic ideas but can evoke both individual recollections, but also shared and culturally embedded forms of nostalgia. This makes perfume a dangerous, unpredictable and underestimated tool. The following essay examines how perfume acts as this higher medium through the biblical myth of the Garden of Eden, approached here not as a historical claim, but as a symbolic and archetypal narrative that articulates our all true origin, loss, and desire. The essay interprets the shared and collective memory of Eden as a desirable and nostalgic longing we have to our place of origin and in some way of home. In this context, nostalgia is understood not merely as personal reminiscence, but as a deeper longing shaped by shared myths, collective memory, and a sense of loss that exceeds individual experience. Further I explore the role of scents in the bible and how scent allows humans to momentarily reconnect with this lost condition of Eden across psychological, philosophical and theological perspectives. “And the Lord God planted a garden eastward in Eden; and there he put the man whom he had formed.” (Gen. 2.8) To understand perfume as a medium of nostalgia and transcendence, it is necessary to first return to the biblical origin of loss itself. Before the Fall, Adam and Eve inhabit Eden as beings unbound by matter. Eden, from a more philosophical perspective, is not simply a divine garden of abundance, but symbolizes a non-material timeless state of existence. In this prelapsarian condition, referring to the time before the fall of Adam and Eve, the human is not yet subjected to entropy. In this state of existence there is no decay because there is no time and there is no death because there is no material body to deteriorate. However, the act of eating the forbidden fruit marks a rupture, destroying this perfect ideal and changing the trajectory of existence and life forever. Persuaded by the serpent who claims that sinning will not kill them but open their eyes and place them on an equal level as the gods, Eve, followed by Adam, chooses knowledge and with it self-awareness. God’s punishment is not only moral exile but, looking at it from the philosophical view once more, transformation. Humanity is expelled from Eden and thrust into a material world governed by time and mortality. This punishment becomes the two conditions of reproduction and survival in the cycle of life. Most crucially, the human body becomes temporal and starts decaying. From this moment on, existence is defined by loss. Matter inevitably deteriorates itself, and time becomes the silent force that carries everything towards death. Summarized, Eden represents a world of perfect harmony, and a time before time. A state without materiality and immortality. Eden therefore symbolizes the home we were promised and the ideal that once was ours but no longer exists. And this ideal is so deeply embedded within us, so woven into our idea of existence, that we long for it. This longing of Eden, is a form of collective nostalgia. With nostalgia I am referring to the widely known phenomenon that can be best described as a longing towards something in the past. The word nostalgia is almost always in reference to a certain type of melancholy, as it is the longing or “missing” we have towards something positive in the past that no longer is in the present. Nostalgia in its essence is therefore the emotional response we have towards a memory, as a memory in itself is neither good or bad, but simply a human psychological aspect of recalling past happenings. However, nostalgia interprets the past as a loss, a desirable state we idealize in the way what we can not have or can not return to. The Garden of Eden can be symbolized as an archetype giving reason to our human experiences of loss and desire. Following Jung’s theory of archetypes, Eden is a memory shared by our collective unconscious and thus acts as an outline for understanding the world and our existence within it. Understanding desire is the greatest way to explain why we yearn for Eden. In psychology, a basic experience of lack is thought to be the source of desire. Desire endures because something is lacking that cannot be completely satisfied, as opposed to being motivated by fulfilment. According to Jacques Lacan, desire is a reaction to a loss or absence of a tangible object rather than the pursuit of one. The state of completion that the thing seems to promise is what is desired, not the object itself. It is actually only after being expelled from Eden that this place became desirable, as the desire comes from the act of missing it rather than wanting or pursuing it in the present. According to Freud, we dedicate our life to chasing a nostalgic satisfaction that we will never find again. This phenomenon is pushed by fantasy and linked to experiences that we might have lived at the beginning of our life and that marks a hole in our lives in the present moment. This feeling is constantly in tension with reality and creates a movement of perpetual insatisfaction pushing us to seek for that lost object. This shared nostalgia for Eden persists not because humanity remembers Eden directly, but because the psychological structure it represents remains active. The desire for Eden continues to orient itself toward an imagined state of harmony, innocence, and completeness. Eden becomes the unconscious reference point for this longing, which is deeply embedded within cultural and psychological frameworks. In this sense, to put it into clear terms, the collective nostalgia for Eden is not about returning to the past, but about expressing the enduring human desire for a state of being that precedes loss itself. My focus of interest lies in the trigger of this nostalgia of Eden through smell. Although memory in general can be elicited through all our senses: an old photo we find in an album or a song from our childhood, memories elicited through smell are rarely neutral. They are more often charged with nostalgia, longing, comfort and loss, since smell is directly linked to memory and emotions. Scents, for instance, are strongly linked to autobiographical memory, a phenomenon often referred to as the Proust effect. “And soon, mechanically, oppressed by the gloomy day and the prospect of a sad future, I carried to my lips a spoonful of the tea in which I had let softened a piece of Madeleine. But at the very instant when the mouthful of tea mixed with cake- crumbs touched my palate, I quivered, attentive to the extraordinary thing that was happening in me. A delicious pleasure had invaded me, without my having any notion as to its cause. I had ceased to feel I was mediocre, contingent, mortal.” (Proust 47). This intimacy shaped the narrative and influenced the use of objects that can only be sensed through smell, such as perfumes. For example, scent has long been associated with death and spirituality. Incense and fragrant resins have long been used in religious ceremonies, funerals, and burial procedures to honour the deceased and direct souls to the divine. In churches, scent operates as an invisible mediator between the earthly and the sacred, creating an atmosphere that opens toward transcendence and brings us closer to the supernatural, to the heavenly. At the same time, perfume has further served a more pragmatic function: masking the pervasive smell of rot, disease, and death. During plagues, for instance, people carried scented cloths to protect themselves from the floating stench of death that filled the streets. From this dual function of spiritual elevation and concealment of decay, emerges the idea that the base note of every perfume is death. Humans do not smell perfume merely to hide decay but we smell scent against decay as an unconscious response to forget the world around us. Smell is therefore both present and absent at the same time. It is in between theoretical senses and practical ones, yet however intangible and complicated, it is the fastest and most powerful trigger of memory. Given the history and spiritual use and understanding of perfume, perfume exists as a vessel between us and the loss of Eden. It gives us the taste of a unity we once belonged to and elicits this ingrained desire towards Eden. As mentioned, this desire exists because Eden no longer exists, and what perfume offers is not a return to Eden, but a sensory articulation of the lack itself: a trace of something absent, and a promise that is never fulfilled.
